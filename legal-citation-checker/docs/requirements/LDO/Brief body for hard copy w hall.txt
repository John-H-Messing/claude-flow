Statement of the issues presented for review1. Whether the Defendant should have been granted a third point for acceptance of responsibility.2. Whether the guilty plea was involuntary because Defendant did not understand possible defenses of duress and necessity. 3. Whether defense counsel rendered ineffective assistance of counsel by failing to investigate and raise a possible defense of duress or necessity.
Statement of the Case     Defendant Gallegos-Rocha was charged in a one count indictment with illegal re-entry after deportation. He did not enter a plea agreement but pled guilty to the indictment. At the change of plea he stated to the Magistrate during the allocution:Ň12						I would like to let you know the only  13 reason I did this is because of the problems I was facing there. I had 14 been in Juarez. I had been kidnapped twice -- No. I mean to say, I 15 was assaulted twice, attacked twice already.Ó Record of Transcript, (here inafter ŇRTÓ) 9/8/2010, p. 11, lines 12-15, ER, p. 17-18.	The Magistrate treated the statement as a matter for sentencing only and advised the Defendant to let the District Court know about but did not inquire as to the DefendantŐs understanding of duress or necessity as a possible defense to the charge and eliciting his waiver of them. The Magistrate then pressed the Defendant to declare his intention with regard to pleading guilty, which he did. Id at RT, pp.11-12, ER, pp. 17.     The PSR, dated December 21, 2010 with addendum dated January 19, 2011, added 16 points for a prior felony conviction for alien smuggling, assessed a base offense level of 8 points and subtracted 2 points for acceptance of responsibility for a total offense level of 22 points. Criminal history was computed as Category II, which translated into a sentencing range of 46 to 57 months. See AppellantŐs Documents Under Seal, Pre-Sentence Report (hereinafter ŇPSRÓ).     The defense requested a third level for acceptance of responsibility but the prosecutor did not move for one and defense counsel acknowledged that the issue was foreclosed by Ninth Circuit case law and stated that the request was made solely to preserve the issue for appeal. The defense also objected to the sentencing range because it overstated the seriousness of the current offense, in that the alien smuggling predicate conviction was decades old.  The defense also argued for a downward departure on the basis of cultural assimilation under USSG ¤2L1.2, note 8 and a combination of extraordinary mitigating circumstances arising from the DefendantŐs life in Mexico following deportation and the impact on his family, which also had tragic circumstances. Objection to Pre-Sentence Report, DE No. 21, ER pp. 22-30.     At sentencing, the District Court entered a plea of guilty, again without consideration of the DefendantŐs understanding of a possible defense of duress or necessity, overruled the objection to the pre-sentence report based upon the failure to award a third point for acceptance of responsibility, affirmed the Guideline calculations of the PSR, granted a downward departure for staleness of offenses and cultural assimilation for a total of three levels, which reduced the sentencing range from 46 to 57 months to 33 to 41 months. The judge was concerned that there had been a relatively recent (2005) conviction for illegal re-entry and so refused to depart further from the Guidelines range as reduced by the three levels and sentenced Mr. Gallegos-Rocha to 33 months in prison with credit for time served and a 3 year period of supervised release, which was a sentence significantly below the Guideline range of the PSR. Transcript of sentencing hearing, 01/21/2011, ER, pp. 35-48.     Defendant timely appealed.
Statement of Facts	The PSR reported that the Defendant Gallegos-Rocha was found by the Border Patrol in the United States near the international boundary on April 28, 2010 as part of an interdicted group of aliens who had been spotted by surveillance camera. Defendant admitted to the officer that he was a citizen of Mexico not in possession of immigration documents allowing him to enter and stay in the U.S. legally, and that he had entered illegally earlier that day.  See  PSR, p. 3.      A records search revealed that the defendant had been deported on May 9, 2006 subsequent to a 1984 conviction for alien smuggling.      Mr. Gallegos-Rocha accepted responsibility for his role in the offense and pled guilty without a plea agreement.     
Summary of the Argument1. There is no basis for a third point of acceptance under Ninth Circuit Law in the absence of a motion by the prosecution for one, which did not occur in this case.2. There is an arguable claim that the plea was not voluntary because the Defendant was not made aware of a possible duress or necessity defense, but the record is not sufficiently developed on direct appeal to justify overturning the conviction and remanding the case given the multitude of reasons advanced for the re-entry, and the fact that no motion was made to set aside the plea before sentencing or made below and harmless error should apply.3. There is an arguable claim of ineffective assistance of counsel in failing to develop the issues of duress or necessity and advising the client of the possible defense prior to the change of plea, but the record is not developed sufficiently at this stage to be able to consider and rule upon it.
Argument 1.  Third Point of Acceptance .A. Standard of Review     A district court's application of the federal Sentencing Guidelines is reviewed de novo. United States v. Nielsen, 371 F.3d 574, 582 (9th Cir. 2004).  ŇWe also review a district court's interpretation of the Sentencing Guidelines de novo. See United States v. Hughes, 282 F.3d 1228, 1230 (9th Cir. 2002).Ó United States v. Auld, 321 F.3d 861 (9th Cir. 2003).      ŇWe review for clear error a district court's decision to reduce a defendant's sentence for acceptance of responsibility, but review de novo whether it misapprehended the law with respect to acceptance of responsibility. United States v. Espinoza-Cano, 456 F.3d 1126, 1130 (9th Cir.2006)Ó. U.S. v. Medina-Beltran, 542 F.3d 729 (9th Cir., 2008), cert. denied 130 S.Ct. 168 (2009), n. 1.B. Applicable Law     In U.S. v. Medina-Beltran, 542 F.3d 729 (9th Cir., 2008), cert. denied 130 S.Ct. 168 (2009),  the Ninth Circuit held that where the government had anticipated and defended against a defendantŐs appeal of his sentence, the government's decision not to move for the additional third point level reduction was not an abuse of discretion. In United States v. Johnson, 581 F.3d 994, 1002 (9th Cir. 2009), the Ninth Circuit reached the same result, because as here the defendant did not waive his right to appeal in the District Court .  U.S. v. Peters, 222 F.3d 111 (D. Fla. 2008)     C. Applied to this Case       Defense counsel candidly admitted that she knew of the existence of the Ninth Circuit case law and had simply raised the issue to preserve it for appeal in the event the case was overturned, but there has been no change in the Ninth Circuit case law and so this is not a valid ground for an appeal.
Argument 2.   Voluntariness of Guilty Plea in Light of Possible Duress And Necessity DefensesA. Standard of ReviewĘĘĘĘ	Adequacy of a Rule 11 hearing is a question of law reviewed de novo. United States v. Jaramillo-Suarez, 857 F.2d 1368, 1369 (9th Cir.1988).Voluntariness of a plea is also reviewed de novo. United States v. Turner, 881 F.2d 684, 685 (9th Cir.), cert. denied, 493 U.S. 871 (1989).B. Applicable Law     The trial judge is required to ascertain whether or not the defendant has knowingly and voluntarily pled guilty because such a plea involves the waiver of certain constitutional and statutory rights. See United States v. Wessells, 936 F.2d 165, 167 (4th Cir.1991); United States v. Navarro-Botello, 912 F.2d 318, 320 (9th Cir.1990).      Rule 11 does not mandate any particular form for the colloquy between the trial judge and the defendant, so long as the judge determines that the defendant made a voluntary decision based on an understanding of both the nature of the charges against him and the statutory and constitutional rights he is relinquishing. See McCarthy v. United States, 394 U.S. 459, 465, 89 S.Ct. 1166, 1170, 22 L.Ed.2d 418 (1969) ( "[A]lthough the procedure embodied in Rule 11 has not been held to be constitutionally mandated, it is designed to assist the district judge in making the constitutionally required determination that a defendant's guilty plea is truly voluntary.") (footnotes omitted), quoted in United States v. Bruce, 976 F.2d 552, 559 (9th Cir.1992).C. As Applied to the Plea     The defendant clearly did not understand that he might have had a theoretical defense on the merits to the charge of illegal re-entry based on a defense of duress or the related defense of necessity. He stated during the colloquy that he had been assaulted in Mexico twice and indicated he had fled to the United States because of the attacks. This arguably raised a colorable defense of duress or necessity that the Magistrate and defense counsel may have failed to spot and of which Mr. Gallegos-Rocha was unaware.      However, the possible defense is not sufficiently developed in the context of a direct appeal to warrant reversal. First, duress and necessity are affirmative defenses with a burden upon the defense to establish it by a preponderance of the evidence. The defendant must also make a prima facie showing before he can even be permitted to testify as to the factual basis at trial. United States v. Vasquez-Landaver, 527 F.3d 798 (9th Cir., 2008); see United States v. Leal-Cruz, 431 F.3d 667 (9th Cir., 2005); United States v. Solorzano-Rivera, 368 F.3d 1073 (9th  Cir., 2004); United States v. Verduzco, 373 F.3d 1022 (9th  Cir., 2004); United States v. Contento-Pachon, 723 F.2d 691 (9th Cir. 1984). Cf. United States v. Sandoval-Gonzalez (9th Cir., 2011).     While a defendantŐs Ňburden is to show that proper advice Ôcould have at least plausibly motivated a reasonable person É . not to have pled guiltyŐÓ, United States v. McTiernan, 546 F.3d 1160 (9th Cir., 2008), citing United States v. Garcia, 401 F.3d 1008, 1011-12 (9th Cir. 2005), there are indications in the record that the defense would not be available to the Defendant, as in the objection to the pre-sentence report, defense counsel stated that a daughterŐs illness also caused him to return illegally, and at sentencing defense counsel alluded to a need for his services in the family business in the United States, citing references to the Pre-Sentence Report. See ER, p. 24, lines 22-26. See also ¦ 7, PSR. In the Sentencing Memorandum, defense counsel cited letters that had been submitted to the Court to argue not only the health concerns of the daughter, and the financial plight of the family and its Mexican restaurant, but also matters of concern about his aging parents. Although violence in Mexico and demands of Mexican mafia upon his business there were mentioned in passing, they were not the sole or primary reason given for his illegal re-entry. See ER, pp. 31-33.     "Fair and just reasons for withdrawal include inadequate Rule 11 plea colloquies, newly discovered evidence, intervening circumstances, or any other reason for withdrawing the plea that did not exist when the defendant entered his plea." United States v. Ortega-Ascanio, 376 F.3d 879, 883ĘĘĘ(9th Cir. 2004). "While the defendant is not permitted to withdraw his plea 'simply on a lark, ' the 'fair and just standard' is generous and must be applied liberally." McTiernan, 546 F.3d at 1167 (quoting United States v. Hyde, 520 U.S. 670, 676-77 (1997)). Thus, "a defendant does not have to prove that his plea is invalid in order to establish a fair and just reason for withdrawal before sentencing." United States v. Davis, 428 F.3d 802, 806 (9th Cir. 2005); see also United States v. Garcia, 401 F.3d 1008, 1012 (9th Cir. 2005) ("[We have] squarely rejected the proposition that the fact that a plea is voluntary, knowing, and intelligent forecloses an attempt to withdraw it prior to sentencing."). Nor must the defendant "proclaim his innocence." Garcia, 401 F.3d at 1012.  But see United States v. Bonilla, No. 09-10307 (9th Cir., 3/11/2011).     Furthermore, while a District CourtŐs findings are reviewed for clear error on appeal from a denial of a motion to withdraw a guilty plea, United States v. McTiernan, 546 F.3d 1160, 1166 (9th Cir. 2008), no motion to withdraw the plea was actually made before sentencing or otherwise, and no objection to its voluntariness was timely raised in the District Court. Even assuming there was error, reversal could also be precluded under the doctrine of harmless error. An error is harmless if it is Ňhighly probable that the error did not affect the judgment.Ó Gov't of Virgin Islands v. Toto, 529 F.2d 278, 284 (3d Cir. 1976)(citation omitted).      
Argument 3. Ineffective assistance of counselA. Standard of Review     ŇWe review a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel de novo. See United States v. Blaylock, 20 F.3d 1458, 1464-65 (9th Cir. 1994).Ó United States v. Rivera-Sanchez, 222 F.3d 1057 (9th Cir., 2000)B. Applicable Law     ŇTypically, claims of ineffective assistance of counsel are inappropriate on direct appeal and should be raised, instead, in habeas corpus proceedings. See United States v. Ross, 206 F.3d 896, 900 (9th Cir. 2000). We will only review ineffective assistance claims on direct appeal where the record is Ôsufficiently developed to permit review and determination of the issue,Ő or Ôthe legal representation is so inadequate that it obviously denies a defendant his Sixth Amendment right to counsel.Ő Id. (quoting United States v. Robinson, 967 F.2d 287, 290 (9th Cir. 1992)) (internal quotations omitted).Ó United States v. Rivera-Sanchez, 222 F.3d 1057 (9th Cir., 2000).   	ĘWhere the record is sufficient to permit direct review upon an appeal from a conviction, to establish ineffective assistance of counsel, the defendant must show that: "(1) his attorney's performance was unreasonable under prevailing professional standards; and (2) that there is a reasonable probability that but for counsel's unprofessional errors, the result would have been different." United States v. Blaylock, 20 F.3d 1458, 1465 (9th Cir. 1994) (quoting Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 694 (1984)) (internal quotations and citations omitted).C. Applied to the Case     Whether defense counsel failed to appreciate the possibility of a duress/necessity defense, or considered and rejected it as a practical matter in light of the conflicting or multiple reasons put forth by the defendant for his returning to the United States cannot be discerned on this record and so it is not ripe for consideration on direct appeal. Whether defense counsel rendered highly effective assistance of counsel, in light of the downward departure from the Sentence Guidelines she was able to persuade the District Court judge to grant her client, or ineffective assistance in light of a possible duress/necessity defense is impossible to determine without further investigation into the representation and attorney-client relationship.1
